Can we share device telemetry safely?

We compared training-based (DP-SGD) and training-free (Private Evolution) approaches for differentially private synthetic data on multi-table device telemetry, and tested utility using a production-style SQL benchmark.

Why This Matters

Device telemetry data (usage patterns, hardware specs, network activity) is valuable for product teams but contains sensitive information about individuals. Sharing raw telemetry poses privacy risks. Differential privacy enables us to release synthetic data that is statistically useful while guaranteeing individual privacy.

  • Privacy risk: Raw telemetry can reveal device models, software versions, usage habits, and locations.
  • Solution: Generate synthetic data that mimics real distributions under privacy guarantees.
  • Trade-off: Adding privacy noise reduces utility. We measure this trade-off across different approaches.

Key Findings

  • Per-table DP-SGD and MST baseline each passed 6 out of 21 benchmark queries.
  • Wide-table DP-VAE and Private Evolution were evaluated on 8 queries (schema constraints), passing 1/8 and 2/8 respectively.
  • Join-heavy aggregate queries were hardest: sparsity in wide-table synthesis and per-table independence breaking cross-table relationships.
  • Histogram-based methods (MST) perform well on simple distributions but struggle with complex joins.
